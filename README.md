Le Taux de Change d'Equilibre dans la CEMAC
 
 Overview
This repository hosts a thematic study published by the Bank of Central African States (BEAC), titled Le taux de change d'equilibre dans la CEMAC: Concepts, Mesures et Implications (BEAC ET N degree 02/20). The paper is authored by : Bomba Mbida Frederic Joseph, Bikai Jacques Landry, Keungne Kouotang Leo Spencer, Mustafa Taher Mohamad, Onomo Betsama Julie, Mambou Patrick Felicien, and Ngomba Bodi Francis Ghislain. The document was released as part of BEAC's Direction Generale des Etudes, Finances et Relations Internationales, dated 4 November 2022.
The views expressed in the paper belong solely to its authors and do not necessarily reflect the official position of BEAC.
 
 Context
The CFA Franc used by the six member countries of the Economic and Monetary Community of Central Africa (CEMAC) is pegged to the euro. This peg has repeatedly triggered debate about whether the currency is overvalued, especially given episodes of strong euro appreciation against the US dollar and the sharp drop in oil prices after 2014, which reduced foreign exchange reserves for oil exporting CEMAC members. The paper revisits this debate by examining whether the CFA Franc, and the CEMAC currency more broadly, has been systematically and persistently misaligned relative to its equilibrium value.
 
 Objective
The central goal of the study is to estimate the equilibrium real effective exchange rate for CEMAC countries and to measure the resulting misalignments (overvaluation or undervaluation episodes). The authors set out to determine whether these misalignments are persistent over time, since persistent misalignment would call for a monetary or price adjustment to preserve the sustainability of the fixed exchange rate regime, while non persistent, alternating misalignment would not require such an adjustment. The work also aims to enrich the set of indicators available to the central bank and to contribute to the broader discussion on the future of the CFA Franc.
 
 Methodological Approach
The authors combine a literature review with two empirical estimation strategies applied to a panel of 14 Franc Zone countries covering the period 1995 to 2016, although the commented results focus specifically on the six CEMAC countries: Cameroon, Central African Republic, Congo, Gabon, Equatorial Guinea, and Chad.
 
 Conceptual Review
The paper first reviews the concept of the equilibrium exchange rate and its role in economic policy, distinguishing price competitiveness from structural or quality based competitiveness. It then surveys the main families of methods proposed in the literature for estimating the equilibrium exchange rate:
The absolute and relative purchasing power parity theory introduced by Cassel.
The behavioral equilibrium exchange rate approach (BEER), which links the real effective exchange rate to long run macroeconomic fundamentals.
The fundamental and desired equilibrium exchange rate approaches (FEER and DEER), which target internal and external balance over the medium term.
The natural real exchange rate approach (NATREX), a dynamic model that decomposes the exchange rate path into short, medium, and long run components.
 
 Empirical Estimation
For the BEER approach, the real effective exchange rate is modeled as a function of relative productivity, terms of trade, trade openness, long term capital movements, public expenditure, and domestic investment. The long run relationship is estimated using three panel cointegration techniques: fully modified ordinary least squares (FMOLS), dynamic ordinary least squares (DOLS), and the pooled mean group estimator (PMG). Before estimating this relationship, the authors run first and second generation panel unit root tests (IPS and Pesaran ADF) and panel cointegration tests (Pedroni and Kao) to confirm that a valid long run relationship exists between the real effective exchange rate and its fundamentals.
Misalignment is then computed as the difference between the observed real effective exchange rate and the equilibrium value predicted by the estimated model.
As a robustness check, the authors apply the NATREX approach using a panel autoregressive distributed lag (ARDL) framework estimated with the pooled mean group estimator, again validated through unit root and cointegration testing.
 
 Data Sources
The real effective exchange rate series come from the EQCHANGE database maintained by CEPII. The remaining macroeconomic variables, including GDP per capita, terms of trade, trade openness, official development assistance, public expenditure, and domestic investment, are drawn primarily from the World Bank World Development Indicators database.
 
 Key Findings
Terms of trade and public expenditure emerge as the most reliable determinants of the equilibrium real effective exchange rate across CEMAC countries, while relative productivity plays a smaller and less consistent role.
Overvaluation and undervaluation phases alternate regularly rather than persisting over time, indicating that CEMAC misalignments are not structurally embedded and therefore do not necessarily call for a monetary realignment of the peg.
During the 2014 to 2016 oil price collapse, oil exporting CEMAC countries experienced a noticeable tendency toward overvaluation, reflecting the pressure that falling export revenues placed on external balance.
Country level patterns are heterogeneous. For example, Cameroon shows undervaluation phases at times when other CEMAC members show overvaluation, and misalignment cycles are not synchronized across the sub region.
The NATREX robustness check confirms the BEER results: productivity, public expenditure, and long term capital inflows are statistically significant, and misalignment phases remain non persistent and non synchronized across countries.
 
 Policy Implications
Because misalignments are not persistent and are heterogeneous across member states, a single common monetary policy adjustment is difficult to justify and could be harmful to some countries while helping others. The authors argue that the more durable path toward reducing misalignment and strengthening competitiveness is greater economic diversification, since currently the CEMAC economies remain heavily dependent on natural resource exports and imports, which leaves them vulnerable to external shocks such as oil price swings.
 
 Suggested Future Research
The authors propose that future work could build a dynamic stochastic general equilibrium model to characterize the optimal policy response of the central bank to shocks affecting exchange rate misalignment, since the present analysis is descriptive and does not model the central bank's reaction function directly.
